The 1.1% Problem: Why Stillwater's Operating Reserves Sit Far Below Policy

The FY2025 audit is clean. The reserve behind it is not comfortable. Stillwater's rainy-day cushion has thinned to about a fifth of the level its own School Board says it should hold.

Stillwater Schools Finance Project July 1, 2026 7 min read

Independent School District No. 834 closed its books for the fiscal year ended June 30, 2025 with an unmodified — “clean” — audit opinion from LB Carlson, LLP. On the surface, that is exactly what a district wants to hear. But a clean opinion certifies that the numbers are accurate, not that they are comfortable. And one number in particular deserves the community's attention.

At year end, the district's unassigned General Fund balance — the truly flexible reserve, the money not already restricted, committed, or assigned to some purpose — stood at about $0.96 million. Against roughly $155.9 million in General Fund spending, that is close to 1.1% of expenditures. The School Board's own policy sets a minimum of 5.0%.

How the cushion thinned

The General Fund is the district's primary operating account — salaries, instruction, transportation, building operations. In FY2025 it took in $153.78 million and spent $155.88 million, an operating shortfall of about $2.1 million before financing sources. After a small amount of other financing and a prior-period accounting correction, the fund's total balance fell from a restated $7.95 million to $6.29 million — a net drawdown of about $1.66 million in a single year.

In other words, the headline “$6.3 million in the bank” overstates the district's maneuvering room. The portion available to absorb a mid-year surprise — an enrollment dip, a special-education placement, a snow-heavy transportation year — is under a million dollars.

Why 5% is the line

A fund-balance floor is not bureaucratic caution for its own sake. Districts receive state aid and property-tax revenue on schedules that do not line up neatly with payroll and operating costs, so they need enough cash on hand to bridge the gaps without borrowing. Reserves also cushion the unexpected and signal financial stability to the bond-rating agencies that price the district's debt. Minnesota's own statutory tripwire — “statutory operating debt” under Minn. Stat. 123B.81 — triggers a mandatory state-approved recovery plan when a district's year-end General Fund position falls below −2.5% of operating expenditures.

Stillwater is not in statutory operating debt. But the distance between a 1.1% cushion and a 5% policy floor is the district's clearest financial-health pressure point — and the reason it is running a Priority-Based Budgeting process.

The cross-currents

Two larger forces frame the reserve story. First, state aid dominates the revenue mix: about 66% of General Fund revenue is state formula aid and 29% is local property tax, so enrollment counts and legislative funding decisions drive the budget more than any local lever. FY2026 adds roughly $200 per pupil (+2.75%) to the formula — helpful, but modest against cost growth in special education, transportation, and benefits, the same pressures that pushed FY2025 spending 2.5% over budget.

Second, the district is in the middle of a major capital program. A $174.8 million bond authorization approved by voters in 2023 (the first $100 million issued in February 2024) is being spent on elementary-school replacements, a middle-school expansion, and safety upgrades. That is why the district's all-funds spending balloons in FY2026. It is important to keep the two stories separate: the capital program is debt-financed and largely walled off in the Building Construction Fund, while the reserve problem lives in the operating General Fund. A busy construction site does not refill the operating cushion.

What to watch next

  • The FY2026 close. Does the operating General Fund stop the bleeding, or does the unassigned balance drift lower still?
  • Priority-Based Budgeting for FY2027. The district's stated mechanism for realigning spending and rebuilding reserves — watch whether the adjustments are large enough to move the 1.1% number.
  • Enrollment. With two-thirds of revenue tied to pupil-driven state aid, the ADM trend is the single biggest swing factor.

None of this makes Stillwater a district in crisis. It makes it a district with a clean audit and a thin margin — a combination that is easy to miss if you read only the opinion letter. The reserves are the number to keep your eye on.
