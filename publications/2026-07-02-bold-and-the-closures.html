BOLD and the Breaking Point: How Stillwater Closed Three Elementary Schools

A decade ago, ISD 834 shut Marine, Withrow, and Oak Park elementary schools, citing falling enrollment and budget strain. The decision reshaped the district's politics, went all the way to the Minnesota Supreme Court — and looks very different in light of what came next.

Stillwater Schools Finance Project July 2, 2026 9 min read

Every district has a decision that residents still measure time against — a "before" and an "after." For Stillwater Area Public Schools, that decision has a name: BOLD.

In December 2015, the district unveiled a strategic plan it called Building Opportunities to Learn and Discover. Its most consequential element was blunt: close three of the district's elementary schools — Marine, Withrow, and Oak Park — and consolidate students into larger, "right-sized" buildings. District leaders argued the moves were necessary because enrollment was declining, several schools were operating below capacity, and budget pressure was destabilizing programming.

What followed was one of the most contentious chapters in the district's modern history — and a case study in how school-closure decisions collide with community trust.

The vote

Opposition organized quickly. A community group, 834 VOICE, formed to fight the plan; petitions circulated, lawmakers were drawn in, and a district-commissioned survey found that a majority of residents and parents opposed BOLD. The board pressed on anyway. After a marathon public hearing, on March 3, 2016 the Board of Education voted 5–2 to adopt a resolution closing the three schools.

The lawsuit that reached the Supreme Court

834 VOICE took the fight to court, using a rarely invoked procedure — a writ of certiorari — to challenge the board's decision directly at the Minnesota Court of Appeals. The group raised two questions: whether the district ran a proper closure hearing (it had imposed time limits on speakers), and whether the board had substantial evidence that closing the schools was "necessary and practicable."

On April 3, 2017, the Court of Appeals sided with the district, finding the hearing procedures reasonable and the decision "supported by substantial evidence" of declining enrollment and operating costs. 834 VOICE asked the Minnesota Supreme Court to review that ruling. On May 19, 2017, the high court declined — and the closures became final. Marine, Withrow, and Oak Park closed at the end of that school year.

The courts settled the legality of BOLD. They could not settle the politics of it — and the politics outlived the litigation.

The turn: closing schools, then building them

Here is where the story complicates the original rationale. BOLD was sold on the premise of a shrinking district. Yet within a few years, Stillwater's trajectory told a different story:

  • In November 2021, voters renewed the operating levy and approved the district's first technology levy — together adding roughly $7.4 million a year.
  • In November 2023, voters approved a $175 million building bond — to replace the Lake Elmo and Andersen elementary schools, expand Oak-Land Middle School, and fund safety and security upgrades.
  • By 2024–2026, the district was projecting enrollment growth, buying land for new elementary sites, and reviewing attendance boundaries — with Lake Elmo among the fastest-growing cities in Minnesota.

In other words, less than a decade after closing three elementary schools on the logic of decline, the district was asking voters to fund building elementary schools on the logic of growth. Both things can be true — enrollment fell in some attendance areas while the district's south end (Lake Elmo, Bayport) grew — but the whiplash is exactly why enrollment projections and facilities planning are so politically charged. A closure justified by a ten-year forecast is only as trustworthy as the forecast.

What it cost — and what it taught

The financial case for BOLD was real: consolidating under-enrolled buildings removes fixed operating costs. But the episode also carried costs that never appear on a balance sheet — years of litigation, a durable rupture in community trust, and a lasting wariness toward district projections. When the district returned to voters in 2021 and 2023, it did so in the long shadow of BOLD, and it invested heavily in communication and community process to win those approvals.

Three lessons stand out for anyone watching the district's finances today:

1. Enrollment forecasts are governance, not just math

The whole BOLD case turned on "substantial evidence" of decline. A decade later, the growth story shows how much weight those forecasts carry — and how costly it is when a community stops trusting them.

2. Closures save operating money; buildings cost capital

BOLD addressed the operating budget; the 2023 bond is a capital commitment repaid over 20 years. The district's operating-reserve problem — its unassigned General Fund balance near 1.1% of spending — was not solved by either. The two ledgers move independently, and conflating them is how communities end up surprised.

3. Trust is a budget line

The most expensive thing about BOLD may have been reputational. Levy and bond campaigns run on credibility; a district that spends that credibility on a contested closure has to rebuild it before the next ask.

BOLD is history now — the schools are closed, the lawsuit is over, and a new generation of buildings is on the way. But it remains the clearest window into how Stillwater makes hard decisions, and into the gap between a defensible financial rationale and a community that feels it wasn't heard. As the district navigates today's deficits and its rising per-pupil costs, that window is worth keeping open.
