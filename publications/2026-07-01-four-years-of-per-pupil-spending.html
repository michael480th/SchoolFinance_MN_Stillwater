Spending Up, Federal Aid Down: Four Years of Stillwater's Per-Pupil Numbers

Between FY2021 and FY2025, Stillwater's spending per student rose nearly 26% and pulled away from the statewide average. The drivers were special education and transportation — and the tailwind of federal pandemic money quietly disappeared.

Stillwater Schools Finance Project July 1, 2026 8 min read

The Minnesota Report Card publishes a standardized, comparable measure of what every district spends per student. Lining up five years of it for Stillwater Area Public Schools tells a clearer story than any single audit can: costs are rising fast, the mix of what's rising is specific, and the money paying for it is shifting underneath the district's feet.

The headline: +26% in four years

Total per-pupil spending in the General Operating Fund climbed from $13,743 in FY2021 to $17,272 in FY2025 — a 25.7% increase. Stillwater began the period roughly in line with the statewide average, briefly dipped below it in FY2022, and has widened a gap above it every year since. By FY2025 the district spent about $1,007 more per student than the typical Minnesota district.

What's rising: special education and transportation

The increase is not evenly spread. Breaking the FY2021 and FY2025 per-pupil figures down by program category shows where the pressure is concentrated. Special education, student transportation, and instructional support all grew far faster than regular classroom instruction — while district-level administration was essentially flat.

Ranked by four-year growth. Special education is both large and fast-growing, which is what makes it the district's defining cost story.

Special education stands out because it is both a big number and a fast-growing one. And it is being driven partly by demand: the share of Stillwater students receiving special-education services has risen every year on record, from 17.3% in FY2023 to 20.0% in FY2026 — one in five students.

What's falling: the federal cliff

While costs rose, one revenue stream ran the other way. Federal spending per pupil peaked at $625 in FY2023 — the height of pandemic-relief (ESSER) money — then fell sharply to $335 by FY2025 as those funds expired. Federal dollars now cover under 2% of the district's operating spending, down from nearly 4% at the start of the period.

The ESSER wind-down is a national story, but it lands locally: programs started with one-time federal money must now be absorbed, cut, or shifted onto state and local funds.

What barely moved: enrollment

None of this is a denominator effect. Enrollment has been remarkably stable — the per-pupil rise reflects real cost growth, not a shrinking student count. Average daily membership hovered near 8,200–8,300 across the whole period, while the October headcount has edged up to a record 8,461 in FY2026.

Why it matters

Rising costs, a fast-growing special-education population, and the loss of federal pandemic aid all landed at the same time the district's operating reserve fell to about 1.1% of spending.

Put the pieces together and the pressure behind Stillwater's thin operating reserves comes into focus. Per-pupil costs are climbing faster than the statewide average; the fastest-growing categories — special education and transportation — are largely non-discretionary; and the federal money that padded budgets from FY2021 to FY2023 is gone. With enrollment flat, there is no growth dividend to grow out of it. That combination is exactly what the district's Priority-Based Budgeting process is trying to manage — and it is why the next few budget cycles matter more than any single year's clean audit.
