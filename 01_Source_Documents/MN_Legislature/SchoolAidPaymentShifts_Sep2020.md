## Executive Summary

School aid payment shifts — delays in state aid payments to school districts — are a mechanism used to address short-term state budget deficiencies. Minnesota must maintain a balanced budget and generally cannot borrow for operating purposes. Two mechanisms have been used historically:
1. **Aid payment shift** — delays state aid payments beyond the state fiscal year end (June 30)
2. **Property tax early recognition shift** — requires districts to recognize property tax payments from June settlement in the prior fiscal year, with corresponding reduction in state aid

---

## School Revenue Sources

School districts and charter schools receive revenue from:
- State aid payments: **68%**
- Local property taxes: **22%**
- Federal funds: **5%**
- Fees charged to parents and other schools: **5%**

---

## How Aid Payment Shifts Work

- Minnesota uses **cash-based accounting** for the state budget; school districts use **modified accrual accounting**
- The state can "save" money by delaying aid beyond June 30; the district still recognizes the full aid entitlement in its fiscal year (accounts receivable)
- Shifts **delay but do not reduce** school revenue
- Each 1 percentage point reduction in current-year aid payment percentage saves the state approximately **$100 million** (one-time)

### Normal Year Aid Payment Structure
- State has paid **90% of current year aid entitlement** in the current state fiscal year (since FY2013)
- Remaining **10% (cleanup payment)** paid in the subsequent fiscal year
- At 90/10: state appropriation = 90% of current year + 10% of prior year = 100% of entitlement

---

## Property Tax Early Recognition Shift

- Requires districts to recognize a portion of June property tax settlement in the **prior** fiscal year
- State then delays aid payments in the same amount
- One-time budget savings to state
- Not all levies are shiftable; debt service levies have specific due dates
- Charter schools are **unaffected** (they don't receive property taxes)
- Savings are not linear: 1% shift ≈ $14M; 10% ≈ $151M; 30% ≈ $566M; max ~50% ≈ $980M

---

## Impact on School Districts

- Districts do not lose revenue, but lose **cash flow**
- May need short-term borrowing to pay bills
- School districts may issue **tax and aid anticipation certificates** (general obligation notes, ~13-month)
- Charter schools cannot issue G.O. notes; must use bank lines of credit at higher interest rates

## Implications for ISD 834

- When the state imposes an aid payment shift, ISD 834 may need to issue tax and aid anticipation certificates to cover operating expenses
- The district's general obligation borrowing capacity and existing cash reserves determine whether a shift requires borrowing
- During periods of state budget stress, aid shifts can impose indirect costs (interest expense) on the district without any reduction in the district's total revenue entitlement
- Once state fiscal conditions improve, the shift is reversed and the state's appropriations exceed entitlements in that year
