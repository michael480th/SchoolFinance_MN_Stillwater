## Property Tax Overview

### Two Property Tax Bases Used for School Levy Calculations

**Referendum Market Value (RMV):**
- Estimated market value, excluding agricultural land/buildings and seasonal recreational residential
- Used for: operating referendum, local optional, transition, and equity levy calculations

**Adjusted Net Tax Capacity (ANTC):**
- = (Taxable Market Value × Statutory Class Rate) / Sales Ratio
- Used for all other school levies including debt service levies
- For LTFM: variation excluding ½ value of agricultural land is used to calculate state/local shares

### Property Tax Terminology
- **Taxable Market Value** = Estimated Market Value – Market Value Exclusion
  - Pay 2024 exclusion: 40% of first $76,000 of value, less 9% of value over $76,000 (no exclusion above $413,800)
  - Pay 2025+: 40% of first $95,000, less 9% of value over $95,000 (no exclusion above $517,200)
- **Net Tax Capacity** = Taxable Market Value × Class Rate
- **Sales Ratio** = Estimated Market Value / Actual Sales Price (computed by Revenue Dept.)
- **ANTC** = Net Tax Capacity / Sales Ratio

### Property Tax Class Rates (FY 2025)

| Property Type | Value Tier | Class Rate |
|--------------|-----------|------------|
| Residential Homestead | First $500,000 | 1.00% |
| Residential Homestead | Remainder | 1.25% |
| Agricultural Land and Buildings | First $2,150,000 | 0.50% |
| Agricultural Land and Buildings | Over $2,150,000 | 1.00% |
| Commercial and Industrial | First $150,000 | 1.50% |
| Commercial and Industrial | Remainder | 2.00% |
| Seasonal Recreational Residential | First $500,000 | 1.00% |
| Seasonal Recreational Residential | Remainder | 1.25% |

### Property Tax Relief Mechanisms
1. **Direct State Aid** programs
2. **Equalization** — state pays portion of levy based on district tax capacity relative to students; ratio of residential pupil units to ANTC valuation
3. **Tax Credits** — state pays portion of homeowner property tax (e.g., School Building Bond Agricultural Credit)

### Property Taxes Payable 2024 Statewide ($13.013 Billion):
- Residential Homestead: ~50%
- Agricultural: ~19%
- Other Residential: ~15%
- Commercial/Industrial: ~12%
- Seasonal Recreational: ~4%

Note: Charter schools do **not** receive property tax revenue.

## Goals and Principles of School Finance

### Goals
- Provides stable, predictable, sustainable revenues over time
- Allocates resources through understandable statewide formulas rationally related to educational need
- Minimizes burdensome paperwork
- Provides incentives and flexibility to increase achievement and close gaps

### Four Funding Principles
1. Goals of a school funding system
2. **Adequacy of Education** — all LEAs have resources to provide adequate basic education regardless of geography or demographics
3. **Equity for Students** — equity for students with different challenges, in different areas, and regardless of program choice
4. **Equity for Taxpayers** — tax burden to provide adequate basic funding is uniform throughout state; additional revenue equally available regardless of location (state aid or equalized levy)

## Legal Context

- **Minnesota Constitution, Article 13, Section 1**: "…it is the duty of the legislature to establish a general and uniform system of public schools. The legislature shall make such provisions by taxation or otherwise as will secure a thorough and efficient system of public schools throughout the state."
- **Skeen Decision (1993)**: MN Supreme Court interpretation

## Pupil Counting System

### Average Daily Membership (ADM)
- **Resident ADM**: public school students living within district boundaries
- **Adjusted ADM**: students actually attending the district or charter school

### Pupil Unit Weights
- Full-time kindergarten and elementary pupils: **1.0 pupil unit**
- Secondary pupils (grades 7-12): **1.2 pupil units** (called "weighted ADM" or adjusted pupil units)
- **Adjusted pupil units** = primary count used for most MN school funding formulas

## Major Components of the School Finance System

Minnesota's system has **more than 40 aid and levy programs.** Key programs:

1. **General Education Revenue** — main vehicle for operating needs
2. **Special Education Revenue**
3. **Voter-approved Operating Revenue** (operating referendum)
4. **Voter-approved Revenue for Building Projects**

### General Education Revenue — $9.963 Billion FY 2025
Components:
- **Basic Revenue** (formula allowance)
- **Compensatory Revenue**
- **English Learner Revenue**
- **Local Optional Revenue (LOR)**
- **Sparsity Revenue**
- Smaller components (integration, transportation sparsity, etc.)

**FY 2025 Special Education Cost & Revenue (from 2023 Cross Subsidy Report):**

| | Amount |
|--|--------|
| Special Education Estimated Total Costs | $3,240,700,000 |
| State Special Education Aid (less ADSIS and non-special ed transportation) | $2,375,100,000 |
| Federal Aid | $218,700,000 |
| Third Party/Medical Assistance Revenue | $72,000,000 |
| **Total Categorical Revenue** | **$2,665,800,000** |
| Unadjusted Gap (Unadjusted Cross Subsidy) | $574,900,000 |
| **Adjusted Cross Subsidy** (allocates some gen ed revenue) | **$457,300,000** |

Key points:
- Funded primarily through state aid with modest federal share
- Significant share of costs is **unfunded** — must be paid from district's general fund
- **Special education tuition bill-back**: resident districts responsible for most unfunded special ed costs of open-enrolled and charter school students

## Enrollment Trends

- Overall enrollment in MN schools projected to **decline** for the foreseeable future
- Greater share of students projected to live in Twin Cities metro area
- Growing shares of: low-income students, English learner students, special education students, students of color
